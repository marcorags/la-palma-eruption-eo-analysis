# Limitations

This document defines the main methodological, data, and reproducibility limitations of the La Palma 2021 Earth Observation workflow.

The limitations below do not invalidate the analysis. They specify how the outputs should be interpreted and which claims the repository does **not** make.

## 1. Partial reproducibility and data availability

The public repository does not include:

- the canonical 204-band unified datastack;
- the preprocessed Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 NetCDF inputs;
- the Copernicus DEM-derived terrain raster;
- the ESA WorldCover raster;
- the professor-provided `Lava_Extent` auxiliary dataset;
- the optional OpenStreetMap GeoPackages;
- large generated raster arrays and model binaries.

As a result, neither notebook can be executed end-to-end from the tracked GitHub files alone.

Users must provide either:

1. the canonical unified datastack and its metadata table to run the downstream analysis; or
2. the complete set of required preprocessed inputs to rebuild a candidate datastack.

The repository should therefore be understood as a documented and inspectable implementation of the workflow, rather than a self-contained data release.

See [`data/README.md`](../data/README.md) and [`DATA_PIPELINE.md`](DATA_PIPELINE.md) for the expected local data layout and the reproducibility boundary.

## 2. Optical observations and cloud obstruction

Sentinel-2 validity masks identify pixels for which all required reflectance bands are finite. They are not complete cloud masks.

Clouds, cloud shadows, haze, and mixed pixels can:

- hide active or recently affected surfaces;
- reduce the number of usable observations;
- delay the first observed change date;
- affect pre/post spectral differences;
- create ambiguity between bright clouds and high-temperature or high-SWIR surface responses.

The downstream workflow explicitly models a `cloud` pseudo-label class and applies conservative spectral rules, but residual cloud contamination and cloud–active-surface ambiguity remain possible.

Because the optical temporal sequence is discrete, the first detected observation represents an observation date, not necessarily the true physical onset of surface change.

## 3. Sentinel-1 evidence is not lava-specific

Sentinel-1 provides cloud-independent observations, but radar backscatter changes are not uniquely attributable to lava emplacement.

VV and VH responses may also be influenced by:

- surface roughness;
- moisture;
- incidence geometry;
- vegetation change;
- topographic effects;
- residual preprocessing artefacts;
- speckle and local noise.

For this reason, SAR is used as supporting temporal and change evidence rather than as a standalone final lava-footprint detector.

The available source variables are labelled `Amplitude_VV` and `Amplitude_VH` and are converted using the project convention `10 × log10(value)`. The source metadata do not independently establish the full radiometric calibration history of these inputs.

## 4. Random Forest validation measures pseudo-label consistency

The Random Forest is trained using conservative rule-based pseudo-labels for:

```text
active_lava
dry_lava
cloud
other
```

The spatial block split reduces direct spatial leakage between training and evaluation samples, but the evaluation labels are generated by the same rule-based pseudo-labelling framework.

Consequently, the reported model metrics measure consistency with the pseudo-label definitions, not independently observed field accuracy.

The Random Forest is therefore used to organize and interpolate spectral evidence across dates. It should not be interpreted as:

- a field-validated land-cover classifier;
- an independently validated lava detector;
- the source of the final affected footprint;
- evidence that the pseudo-label rules are physically complete.

Any bias or omission in the pseudo-label rules can propagate into the fitted model.

## 5. Dependence on the accepted lava-reference footprint

The final `lava_reference` layer originates from the `Lava_Extent` auxiliary data provided directly by the course professor.

Within this project, it is treated as the accepted final affected-domain footprint. It is used to:

- constrain the complete timing reconstruction;
- define the affected area for land-cover and terrain summaries;
- support diagnostic comparisons;
- delimit optional infrastructure-exposure intersections.

The project does not independently reconstruct or field-validate this final footprint.

Any positional error, omission, commission, or definition mismatch in the reference layer would propagate into area totals, reconstructed timing, and exposure summaries.

Diagnostic agreement with `lava_reference` is therefore agreement with the accepted project reference, not independent validation against field observations or an official damage inventory.

## 6. The timing reconstruction is a constrained proxy, not a physical simulation

The affected-surface timing reconstruction combines:

- sparse optical evidence from the observation dates;
- Random Forest and heat-index timing signals;
- the accepted final lava-reference domain;
- the Tajogaite vent location;
- slope-weighted geodesic ordering;
- isotonic temporal regularization;
- local frontier propagation;
- the known eruption-end constraint.

The resulting arrival-day surface is a spatially coherent **timing proxy** inside the accepted final footprint.

It is not:

- a lava-flow dynamics model;
- a hydraulic or thermodynamic simulation;
- a reconstruction of lava depth, velocity, discharge, or temperature;
- a literal map of active lava on every day;
- an independently observed daily perimeter sequence.

Daily affected-area maps interpolate and constrain incomplete temporal evidence. Their apparent day-level precision should not be interpreted as day-level observational certainty.

Uncertainty is greatest where:

- optical observations are missing or cloud-obstructed;
- no direct spectral timing evidence is available;
- the inferred order is driven mainly by distance, slope, or frontier constraints;
- the accepted final footprint contains disconnected or weakly supported regions.

## 7. Exposure results are proxies, not official damage statistics

ESA WorldCover and optional OpenStreetMap layers are intersected with the accepted lava-reference footprint to estimate affected land-cover classes, buildings, and roads.

These results indicate potential spatial exposure. They do not establish:

- confirmed structural damage;
- building occupancy or use at the time of the eruption;
- road service interruption;
- asset value or vulnerability;
- reconstruction status;
- official compensation or emergency-management counts.

OpenStreetMap completeness, positional accuracy, feature classification, and temporal currency are not independently validated in this project.

A geometric intersection means that a mapped feature overlaps the accepted affected footprint. It does not by itself prove destruction or functional loss.

## 8. Spatial harmonization and resampling

All sources are aligned to a common 10 m `EPSG:32628` grid. This facilitates multi-source analysis but introduces scale and interpolation effects.

Relevant limitations include:

- the approximately 30 m Copernicus DEM-derived layers are resampled to 10 m and do not gain true 10 m terrain detail;
- bilinear interpolation smooths continuous raster values;
- aspect is circular, so bilinear interpolation may be imperfect near the 0°/360° boundary;
- nearest-neighbour resampling preserves categorical classes but can shift class boundaries at the target grid;
- mixed pixels remain possible along lava, land-cover, cloud, coastline, and infrastructure boundaries;
- all area estimates inherit the geometry and resolution of the common grid.

The 10 m output resolution should therefore be interpreted as the analysis grid, not as the native information resolution of every input layer.

## 9. Case-study specificity and transferability

The workflow was designed for the 2021 Tajogaite eruption on La Palma and uses:

- a specific set of Sentinel acquisition dates;
- case-specific spectral rules and thresholds;
- a professor-provided final affected footprint;
- the local topographic setting;
- a fixed common grid and study area.

The implementation demonstrates a transferable analysis structure, but the numerical thresholds, pseudo-label rules, temporal constraints, and performance results should not be assumed to generalize unchanged to other eruptions, sensors, climates, or landscapes.

Application to another event would require renewed data preparation, threshold assessment, reference-data evaluation, and validation.

## 10. Incomplete upstream provenance

The repository does not fully reproduce the raw-data acquisition and sensor-specific preprocessing stages.

The following upstream information is incomplete:

- complete Sentinel product identifiers;
- the original Google Earth Engine workflow and export configuration;
- the complete SNAP processing graph and all operator parameters;
- the full history of intermediate EO products;
- the exact portal and native distribution variant used for the original Copernicus DEM;
- independent confirmation that the Sentinel-1 amplitude-labelled inputs represent calibrated sigma-nought power.

These uncertainties affect full raw-product-to-result reproducibility. They do not change the repository-level harmonization and downstream logic recorded in the notebooks, but they limit independent reconstruction of the original inputs.


## 11. Appropriate interpretation of the outputs

The strongest supported interpretation is that the repository provides:

- a documented multi-sensor EO harmonization workflow;
- optical and SAR change diagnostics;
- a pseudo-label-based organization of spectral evidence;
- a constrained timing reconstruction within an accepted final footprint;
- land-cover, terrain, and infrastructure-exposure summaries.

The repository does **not** claim:

- complete raw-data-to-result reproducibility from GitHub alone;
- field-validated daily lava-flow reconstruction;
- a physically simulated lava propagation process;
- independently validated Random Forest ground-truth accuracy;
- official infrastructure-damage statistics;
- universal transferability to other volcanic events.
